Analyzing Randomized Experiments: Fisher vs. Neyman

Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) are the foundation of causal analysis. By randomly partitioning a sample into a Treatment Group ($W_i = 1$) and a Control Group ($W_i = 0$), we isolate the causal impact of an intervention. Historically, two statisticians proposed fundamentally different ways to analyze this data.

Core Concepts of Permutation Distributions (Fisher) and Conservative Variance Estimation (Neyman)

Fisher's Sharp Null Hypothesis

Ronald Fisher was interested in testing what he called the Sharp Null Hypothesis:

H₀: Yᵢ(1) = Yᵢ(0) for all individuals i

The Conceptual Difference: Fisher's null claims the treatment has **absolutely zero effect on anyone whatsoever**. This represents a pure placebo. Because we assume the treatment does nothing, we solve the missing data problem: the counterfactual potential outcome for each individual under the alternative assignment is identical to their observed outcome.

The Test Procedure: Once we know all potential outcomes, we can simulate what our test statistic (such as the difference in means) would look like under every single possible treatment assignment combination (permutations). We calculate an exact p-value by finding the proportion of these shuffles that yield differences as large or larger than observed.

Neyman's Average Treatment Effect

Jerzy Neyman rejected the sharp null, arguing that treatment effects are likely heterogeneous: some individuals benefit, while others may be harmed. He focused on the **Average Treatment Effect (ATE)**:

τ = E[Yᵢ(1) - Yᵢ(0)]

The Conceptual Difference: Neyman's null is $H_0: \text{ATE} = 0$, meaning the treatment has zero effect **on average**, even if it affects individuals differently.

The Estimation Procedure: Neyman proved that the simple difference in sample averages is an unbiased estimator for ATE. However, because we sample without replacement, the exact variance contains an unobservable term (the variance of individual-level treatment effects). Neyman proposed ignoring this negative term, providing a **conservative standard error** (variance upper bound):

SE = √[ (s_Treatment² / N_Treatment) + (s_Control² / N_Control) ]

Primary Example: The Honey Study

This example uses a subset of six children from a randomized study analyzing honey as a cough treatment. Cough severity is measured on a scale from 0 (no cough) to 6 (severe cough). Under the sharp null ($H_0$), cough severity would be unchanged whether the child received honey ($W_i=1$) or control ($W_i=0$).

Original Case Study: Honey Clinical Trial

In the original clinical trial (Paul et al., 2007), researchers tested the effect of buckwheat honey, dextromethorphan (DM), and no treatment (placebo) on nocturnal cough severity in 105 children. The outcomes showed that honey scored significantly better than no treatment in reducing cough frequency and severity.

For educational simplicity, modern econometrics texts (such as Rubin & Imbens, 2015) select a miniature subset of 6 children to teach Fisher's exact permutation. This illustrates how small-sample clinical outcomes can be analyzed under the strict assumption of a sharp null hypothesis.

Complicated Real-World Case: Teacher Incentives in India

To evaluate Neyman's framework, we analyze a real-world incentive study (Duflo, Hanna, Ryan 2012) addressing high teacher absenteeism. 113 rural schools were randomized:

* **Treatment Group (57 schools):** Teachers were monitored with cameras and paid based on presence (floor salary plus a daily presence bonus).
* **Control Group (56 schools):** Standard monthly salary without camera monitoring.

Original Case Study: Seva Mandir Teacher Incentive Program

In the Seva Mandir study in Rajasthan, India (Duflo, Hanna, Ryan, 2012), schools faced an absenteeism crisis, with teachers absent over 44% of the time. The authors introduced camera monitoring where teachers took photos with students at the start and end of the school day to prove attendance. High attendance led to bonus payouts.

The study demonstrated that camera monitoring cut absenteeism in half, increasing teacher presence from 58% to 80%. Crucially, it also checked the "test attempt rate" on assessment days to rule out selection bias, confirming that the incentive did not prompt weaker students to stay home or teachers to drop them on test days.
