What is Hypothesis Testing?

Hypothesis testing is a formal statistical framework used to decide whether the data at hand support a particular claim or theory. It allows experimenters (like product managers, data scientists, and growth marketers) to make mathematical assertions about populations based on sample statistics.

"Hypothesis testing is the mathematical interface between intuition and evidence."

Why use it?

In business and science, we are prone to confirmation bias. If we change a website button color from blue to green and see conversion increase by 0.5% the next day, we naturally want to celebrate. Hypothesis testing asks: "Could this 0.5% increase simply be random daily fluctuation?" It helps filter out noise from true product signal.

The General Steps:

  1. State the Hypotheses: Define the Null Hypothesis (\(H_0\)) and Alternative Hypothesis (\(H_a\)).
  2. Set Decision Criteria: Choose a significance level (\(\alpha\)), usually 5% (\(0.05\)).
  3. Collect Data & Compute: Gather sample data and calculate the appropriate test statistic (Z or t) and corresponding p-value.
  4. Make a Decision: Reject the Null Hypothesis if the p-value is less than \(\alpha\), or fail to reject it otherwise.

Null vs. Alternative Hypotheses

Every hypothesis test requires setting up two mutually exclusive statements:

Null Hypothesis (\(H_0\))

The hypothesis of "no difference", "no effect", or "no change".

It represents the status quo. If you test a new feature, \(H_0\) assumes the feature has no effect on user behavior.

\(H_0: \mu_{new} = \mu_{old}\)

Alternative Hypothesis (\(H_a\) or \(H_1\))

The hypothesis of "difference", "effect", or "change".

It is what you want to prove. It asserts that the new feature does change user behavior.

\(H_a: \mu_{new} \neq \mu_{old}\)

One-Tailed vs. Two-Tailed Tests

Depending on your alternative hypothesis, you choose the directionality of the test:

  • Two-Tailed (Non-directional): You test if the new version is simply *different* (better or worse). Formulated as: \(H_a: \mu \neq \mu_0\). Rejection region is split between both ends of the distribution curve.
  • One-Tailed (Directional): You test if the new version is *specifically better* (\(H_a: \mu > \mu_0\)) or *specifically worse* (\(H_a: \mu < \mu_0\)). Rejection region is concentrated entirely in one tail.
Note: In A/B testing, two-tailed tests are widely considered the industry standard because they protect against claiming a positive result when in fact the variation is worse.

Balancing the Trade-off

There is a natural trade-off between Type I and Type II errors. If you decrease your willingness to make a Type I error (decreasing \(\alpha\) from 5% to 1%), you increase the threshold required to declare a result significant. This makes it harder to reject the null hypothesis, which in turn increases your probability of committing a Type II error (\(\beta\)), thereby lowering your test power.

The p-value and Alpha (\(\alpha\))

The **p-value** is one of the most misunderstood concepts in statistics. Let's define it precisely:

p-value definition: The probability of obtaining test results at least as extreme as the result actually observed, under the assumption that the null hypothesis is true.

What the p-value is NOT:

  • It is **not** the probability that the null hypothesis is true.
  • It is **not** the probability that the alternative hypothesis is false.
  • It is **not** the probability that the observed result was caused by random chance.

Decision Rule

We compare the p-value to our pre-defined significance level \(\alpha\):

If \(p < \alpha\): Reject \(H_0\)

The probability of observing this data by chance is very low. We conclude there is a statistically significant effect.

If \(p \ge \alpha\): Fail to Reject \(H_0\)

The data is reasonably likely to occur under the null hypothesis. We cannot conclude that an effect exists.

Statistical Power (\(1 - \beta\))

Statistical power is the probability that a test will correctly reject a false null hypothesis. In plain English, **power is the probability of finding an effect, assuming an effect actually exists.**

Why Power Matters

An underpowered test is like looking for a needle in a haystack with a weak magnifying glass. You are likely to walk away concluding there is no needle (Type II error), when in fact it was there all along. In A/B testing, standard practice is to aim for **80% power** (\(\beta = 0.20\)).

Factors that Influence Power:

  1. Sample Size (\(n\)): As sample size increases, random variance decreases. Standard error shrinks, which makes it much easier to detect small differences. (Higher sample size = Higher power)
  2. Effect Size: A massive improvement (e.g. 50% increase in clicks) is much easier to spot than a tiny improvement (e.g. 0.1% increase). (Larger effect size = Higher power)
  3. Significance Level (\(\alpha\)): If you make \(\alpha\) more lenient (e.g. 10% instead of 5%), you widen the rejection region, making it easier to reject the null. (Higher alpha = Higher power, but more false positives!)

Connecting Hypothesis Testing to A/B Testing

A/B testing is simply hypothesis testing applied to digital products. You split your traffic into two groups:

  • A (Control): The existing version of the page or feature.
  • B (Treatment): The new variant you want to test.

Common Practical Pitfalls in A/B Testing:

  1. Peeking (The Multiple Comparisons Problem): Checking the results daily and stopping the test as soon as the p-value dips below 0.05. This dramatically inflates your actual Type I error rate. You must decide on your sample size beforehand and wait until it is complete.
  2. Running too many tests: If you run 20 tests simultaneously with \(\alpha = 0.05\), by pure random chance, you expect at least one test to show a "statistically significant" result, even if all treatments are identical to control.
  3. Confusing statistical significance with practical significance: With a sample size of 10 million users, a 0.01% change in conversion rate might be statistically significant (p < 0.05), but the cost to build and maintain the feature might far outweigh the minor conversion increase. Always calculate the ROI.
